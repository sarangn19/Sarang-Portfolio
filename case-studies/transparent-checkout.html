Transparent Checkout & Pricing System

Reducing price shocks and improving trust in food delivery checkout — a UX case study on pricing clarity.

Product Design UX Research Food Delivery

Why does checkout feel like a trap?

Many food delivery platforms reveal additional charges only during the final stage of checkout. This creates a mismatch between the user's expected price and the actual payment amount, often leading to frustration, cart abandonment, and reduced trust.

The goal of this project was to design a checkout system that communicates clear pricing information earlier in the ordering journey — so users always know what they're paying before they reach the payment step.

Users often feel blindsided at checkout

Users often encounter unexpected price increases during checkout, due to charges that appear only at the final billing step — such as service fees, delivery surcharges, and taxes added late in the flow.

Understanding how users perceive pricing

Secondary research and analysis of existing checkout experiences across multiple platforms. Goal: identify where confusion occurs and how pricing transparency can improve user trust.

Pricing opacity is a trust killer

Food delivery users need a transparent pricing system that clearly communicates the total cost of their order before checkout, because hidden or unexpected fees create confusion and reduce trust in the platform.

Making every rupee visible, every step of the way

The solution introduces a transparent pricing system that provides clear cost visibility throughout the entire ordering journey.

Three principles guided every decision

  • Pricing visible at every step — no surprises before payment

Trust is built one transparent step at a time

Pricing transparency builds trust — seeing pricing information early creates significantly more trust than revealing it at the final step. Even small improvements in how cost information is communicated can significantly influence user behaviour and satisfaction.

Designing financial interactions requires balancing transparency with simplicity — too much data at once overwhelms users. The goal is clarity, not completeness.
