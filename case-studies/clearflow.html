ClearFlow : Designing Against EMI Blindness

Your bank balance tells you how much money you have. It doesn't tell you when you're broke. Flow looks forward — projecting your balance across time so you can see exactly when it runs out, before it does.

RoleLead Designer
Research30 Interviews · 70 Surveys
Qualitative + Usability Testing
MethodsEarly Usability Testing
ClearFlow

Problem

Young professionals often misinterpret their available bank balance as actual savings, without accounting for fixed EMIs and recurring expenses. This leads to poor financial decisions and over-commitment to new loans or installments.

Research

Secondary Research (Indian Context)

In this stage, existing financial management apps used in India were analyzed to understand how users currently track expenses, manage EMIs, and monitor their finances. The goal was to identify what problems these tools solve and what gaps still exist for young Indian professionals. Some commonly used financial and expense management apps in India include: Walnut, CRED, Money Manager, ET Money, Mint. These apps were reviewed based on their expense tracking, EMI visibility, budgeting tools, and financial insights.

Final Problem Statement

Through user interviews and survey analysis, it was discovered that many young professionals struggle to clearly understand their actual monthly savings after accounting for EMIs and daily expenses. Although users frequently check their bank balance, this number often creates a false sense of financial security because it does not reflect upcoming commitments or recurring costs.

As a result, users tend to make financial decisions—such as taking new EMIs or spending on discretionary purchases—without fully understanding how these commitments affect their long-term financial stability. Existing financial tools mainly focus on recording past transactions, rather than helping users predict the future impact of their financial choices.

This gap leads to a phenomenon that can be described as "EMI Blindness," where users commit to multiple installments without having a clear view of their true disposable income or savings.

Therefore, there is a need for a financial planning solution that helps young professionals clearly visualize their real savings, understand the impact of EMIs, and make better spending decisions.

Ideation

How Might We (HMW) Questions

Based on the research insights and defined problem statement, How Might We (HMW) questions were created to reframe the problem into design opportunities. These questions help guide the ideation process by focusing on potential solutions that address users' financial challenges.

HMW questions generated from research insights:

• How might we help users clearly understand their actual savings after paying EMIs and monthly expenses?
• How might we show users how much money they can safely spend without affecting their financial stability?
• How might we help users understand the long-term impact of taking new EMIs before committing to them?
• How might we automatically track small daily transactions such as UPI payments and subscriptions?
• How might we reduce financial stress by providing clearer insights into users' financial health?
• How might we simplify financial tracking for users who do not consistently log their expenses?
• How might we provide a unified view of income, expenses, EMIs, and savings in one dashboard?

Key Solution Ideas

Daily Spending Summary
A daily digest that shows how much money was spent and how it affects the remaining monthly budget.
Automatic Expense Tracking
Automatic categorization of transactions such as UPI payments, subscriptions, and card payments to reduce manual logging.
Safe-to-Spend Balance
A feature that shows users how much money they can safely spend after accounting for EMIs, bills, and savings goals.
